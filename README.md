# CS-255
System Analysis and Design  
Briefly summarize the DriverPass project. Who was the client? What type of system did they want you to design?   
The DriverPass project involved designing a system for a client who wanted to help students prepare for their driving tests by offering online learning materials, scheduling driving lessons, and managing instructor availability. My role was to analyze the client’s needs and create system documentation, including UML diagrams, technical requirements, and a client‑friendly presentation.   
What did you do particularly well?  
One thing I did particularly well was translating the client’s needs into clear diagrams and explanations that a nontechnical audience could understand. I focused on clarity, structure, and making sure each part of the design tied directly back to the requirements gathered from the interview transcript.  
If you could choose one part of your work on these documents to revise, what would you pick?  
If I were to revise one part of my work, I would refine some of the early diagrams to make them more consistent and visually balanced.  
How would you improve it?  
I would also the written explanations to make them even more accessible to stakeholders who may not be familiar with system design.  
How did you interpret the user’s needs and implement them into your system design? Why is it so important to consider the user’s needs when designing?  
To interpret the user’s needs, I relied heavily on the interview transcript and the business requirements from Project One. I identified the core problems the client wanted to solve—such as scheduling lessons, accessing training materials, and managing instructors—and built the system around those priorities. Considering user needs is essential because a system that doesn’t align with real‑world workflows will fail, no matter how well it is built technically.  
How do you approach designing software? What techniques or strategies would you use in the future to analyze and design a system?  
When designing the software for this particular project, I approached it by breaking the system into smaller, understandable parts and modeling how users will interact with each component. Techniques like use case analysis, activity diagrams, and class diagrams helped me visualize the system before any of the code was written. In the future, I will continue using these strategies and skills along with iterative feedback, user stories, and early prototyping to ensure the design stays aligned with user expectations.
